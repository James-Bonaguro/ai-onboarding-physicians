# Setup: Getting Configured (15 minutes, one time)

## What You're Doing

You're going to paste some text into your AI's settings. This text runs behind every conversation. It tells the AI who you are, what you do, and how you work. After this, every response it gives you will be better.

## Step 1: Open Your Settings

**ChatGPT:**
- Click your profile picture (bottom left)
- Click "Customize ChatGPT"
- You'll see two boxes: "What would you like ChatGPT to know about you?" and "How would you like ChatGPT to respond?"

**Claude:**
- Click your profile picture (bottom left)
- Click "Settings"
- Click "Profile"
- You'll see a "Custom Instructions" box

## Step 2: Fill in "About You"

Copy the template below. Replace everything in [brackets] with your actual information. Delete any lines that don't apply.

```
I am a [specialty — e.g., interventional cardiologist, hospitalist, general surgeon] at a [setting — e.g., large academic medical center, community hospital, private practice].

I have [X] years of experience. I [perform procedures / manage inpatients / run an outpatient clinic / all of the above]. My typical procedures include [e.g., diagnostic catheterization, PCI, TAVR, peripheral interventions — or skip this if you're non-procedural].

I also [teach fellows and residents / run a research program / serve on hospital committees / handle administrative work — delete what doesn't apply].

When I write, I write for [audience — e.g., other physicians in referral letters, patients in discharge summaries, trainees in teaching contexts, quality committees]. My documentation style is [concise and structured / detailed narrative / whatever you prefer].

I care most about: evidence-based reasoning, clear clinical decision-making, time efficiency, and staying current with guidelines.

I do NOT want: patient-facing disclaimers like "consult your physician" — I am the physician. No generic medical advice. No hedging every statement. Talk to me like a colleague.
```

## Step 3: Fill in "How to Respond"

This tells the AI how to talk to you. Copy and paste, then adjust to your preference:

```
Be direct. No preamble, no filler, no "Great question!" openers.

When I share clinical reasoning or a management plan, challenge it. Find the gaps in my logic, the differentials I might be missing, the guidelines I might be overlooking. Don't validate me — push back.

Cite specific evidence when available. Use trial names and key findings, not vague references. Distinguish between ACC/AHA Class I, IIa, IIb, and III recommendations when relevant.

When I describe a clinical scenario, think like a colleague — not a textbook and not a medical student.

Keep responses concise unless I specifically ask for depth. I read between cases and between patients. Respect my time.

When you don't have enough information to give a useful answer, ask me what's missing instead of guessing.
```

## Step 4: Test It

Open a new conversation and type something relevant to your practice:

> "I have a 72-year-old with a new diagnosis of atrial fibrillation, CHA2DS2-VASc of 4, and a history of GI bleeding 6 months ago. Help me think through anticoagulation."

**If setup worked:** The response will reference your specialty, think through the problem like a colleague, and cite specific evidence. It won't start with "Atrial fibrillation is an irregular heart rhythm..."

**If setup didn't work:** The response will sound generic, like it could be written for a medical student. Go back and check that your custom instructions saved properly.

## You're Done

This is the foundation. Every conversation from here forward is running on your custom configuration. The five rules you just read will compound on top of this.
