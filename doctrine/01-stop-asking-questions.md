# Rule 1: Stop Asking It Questions. Start Giving It Jobs.

## The Mistake Everyone Makes

When most physicians open ChatGPT or Claude, they type a question:

> "What are the indications for TAVR?"

And they get back a textbook answer. Then they think AI is useless.

## What Actually Works

Instead of asking a question, give it a job:

> "I have a 78-year-old with severe aortic stenosis, bicuspid valve, STS score of 4.2, and heavily calcified iliofemoral arteries on CT. Walk me through the access strategy decision-making for TAVR. Compare transfemoral versus alternative access given this anatomy."

Same topic. Completely different output. The first one gives you UpToDate. The second one gives you something you can actually use to plan your case.

## The Shift

| Stop doing this | Start doing this |
|---|---|
| "What are the guidelines for dual antiplatelet therapy?" | "I have a patient 6 months post-DES who needs non-cardiac surgery. Walk me through the DAPT decision — bleeding risk vs. stent thrombosis — given current ACC/AHA recommendations." |
| "How do I interpret this echo?" | "I'm looking at an echo showing EF 35%, moderate MR, and a dilated LV. The patient is asymptomatic. Help me think through whether this changes my management and what I need to follow up on." |
| "What causes troponin elevation?" | "I have a post-op patient with a troponin bump to 0.8, no chest pain, no ECG changes. Help me work through the differential — type 1 vs. type 2 MI vs. demand ischemia — and what workup makes sense here." |
| "Write me a prior auth letter." | "Draft a prior authorization letter for a drug-eluting stent in a patient with symptomatic multivessel disease. The denial was based on 'medical necessity not established.' Cite the relevant ACC/AHA Appropriate Use Criteria and the class of recommendation." |

## Why This Matters

Google answers questions. AI does work.

When you treat it like Google, you get Google-quality answers — generic, surface-level, not tailored to your patient or your practice.

When you treat it like a sharp fellow sitting next to you, you get analysis, reasoning, and drafts you can actually build on.

## The One-Sentence Rule

**Before you type anything, ask yourself: "Am I asking a question, or am I assigning a task?"**

If it's a question, rewrite it as a task. Every time.
