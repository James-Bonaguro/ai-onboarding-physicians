# Your First Week

## How This Works

Five days. Fifteen to twenty minutes each day. By the end of the week, you'll know whether AI is useful for your practice or not. No theory — just doing.

---

## Day 1: Read the Rules + Set Up (25 minutes)

**Part 1 — Read the five rules (10 minutes):**
Start with [Rule 1](../doctrine/01-stop-asking-questions.md) and read through all five. They're short. They change how you approach every AI conversation from here on.

**Part 2 — Set up your custom instructions (15 minutes):**
Follow the [setup guide](../setup/01-getting-configured.md). Copy the template, fill in your details, paste it into ChatGPT or Claude. You do this once.

**Also read:** [Safe Use](../setup/02-safe-use.md) — know the HIPAA boundaries before you start.

**End of Day 1:** Your AI knows you're a physician. Every conversation from here is different.

---

## Day 2: Ask a Real Clinical Question (15 minutes)

Think of a clinical question you actually have. Maybe it's something you'd normally look up on UpToDate or dig through PubMed for.

**Try it with AI.** But remember Rule 1 — don't ask a question, give it a job:

> **Instead of:** "What's the evidence for SGLT2 inhibitors in heart failure?"
>
> **Try:** "I have a patient with HFrEF, EF 30%, on sacubitril-valsartan and carvedilol. I'm considering adding dapagliflozin. Walk me through the evidence — specifically DAPA-HF — and whether the benefit holds if the patient is non-diabetic. What's the NNT?"

Compare the output to what you'd get from a PubMed search. Notice the time difference.

**Important:** AI has a training data cutoff. For the most recent trials or guideline updates, always verify against UpToDate or the primary source. AI is a synthesis tool, not a live database.

---

## Day 3: Draft a Document (15 minutes)

Pick something you were going to write anyway. A discharge summary. A referral letter. A prior auth. A procedure note template.

Give AI the clinical details (de-identified — see [Safe Use](../setup/02-safe-use.md)) and ask it to draft it.

Then use Rule 4 — iterate. The first draft won't be perfect. Push it:
- "The indication section needs to be stronger for billing."
- "Make the follow-up plan more specific."
- "This is too long. Cut it in half."

**Compare:** How long did this take versus doing it from scratch?

---

## Day 4: Use Voice Mode (15 minutes)

Both ChatGPT and Claude have voice modes. This is where AI gets interesting for physicians.

**Try this on your commute or between cases:**
- Think out loud about a complex case. Let AI be the sounding board.
- Talk through your reasoning for a management plan. See if it pushes back (Rule 5).
- Process a new trial result you read about. Ask it to help you think through whether it changes your practice.

Voice mode is unstructured and fast. It's the AI equivalent of thinking out loud with a colleague in the hallway.

**Remember:** HIPAA applies to voice too. Don't dictate patient names, MRNs, or dates of birth. Use "a patient in their 70s with..." instead.

---

## Day 5: The Pushback Test (15 minutes)

This is the most important day.

Take a clinical decision you've already made — something you're confident about. Tell it to AI. But add this:

> "I want you to challenge this. Find the weakest part of my reasoning. What am I not considering? What would a colleague at conference push back on?"

This is Rule 5 in action. See what it catches. You might be surprised.

**Example:**
> "I have a 70-year-old with 3-vessel disease, EF 45%, and diabetes. I'm recommending CABG based on the FREEDOM trial. Challenge my reasoning — what are the arguments for PCI in this patient, and is there anything about this specific case that makes CABG less clear-cut?"

---

## End of Week: Honest Assessment

Ask yourself three questions:

1. **Did I save time on at least one task?** (documentation, literature review, administrative work)
2. **Did the pushback exercise surface anything I hadn't considered?**
3. **Do I want to keep using this?**

If the answer to any of these is yes, you've found your use case. Build on it.

If the answer is no across the board, you gave it a fair shot. Not every tool is for every person.
